# MoralMachine (2018)

**Dimension**: Human centered  **Source / path**: `/private/tmp/pdf_texts_hc/11292.txt`

## Representative tool

Cross-cultural trolley-style scenario matrix with 9-attribute orthogonal design

## Tool explanation (full)

Paper: MoralMachine constructs a systematic, web-based experimental interface where users confront pairwise moral dilemmas (e.g., sparing pedestrians vs. passengers) across nine orthogonal attributes: perceived age, gender, fitness, social status, species (human/pet), number of lives, role (pedestrian/driver), rule adherence (jaywalking), and randomness. Each scenario is generated by combinatorially sampling from these dimensions, enabling large-scale, cross-national comparison of moral preferences. It relies on crowdsourced global participation (40M+ decisions, 10 languages, 233 countries) and maps responses to Moral Foundations Theory (MFT) constructs.

## What it improves vs. baseline

Moves beyond single-theory or philosopher-centric thought experiments (e.g., classic trolley problem) to a scalable, empirically grounded, multi-attribute behavioral measurement framework that captures cultural variation in moral weighting — not just 'what' people choose, but 'how consistently' they trade off dimensions across populations.

## Adaptation example

In ethical evaluation of AI for elderly care, this matrix tool can be reused to reconstruct scenarios: fix “age” among the 9 attributes as advanced age; map “fitness” to cognitive and mobility capacity; extend “role” to caregiver–care-recipient relationships; and collect preferences from Japanese, Spanish, and Arabic communities to generate culturally adapted benchmarks for care decision trade-offs.

## Self-acknowledged limits

Self-selected online participants; trolley-style scenarios highly stylized; cross-cultural framing limited.

## Synthesis

The review notes that the Moral Machine exhibits strong cross-cultural coverage but is limited to autonomous-vehicle trolley-style scenarios. Subsequent work criticizes it for focusing solely on outcome choices while neglecting procedural reasoning, and for its nine-attribute design, which does not clearly map onto established moral foundations.
